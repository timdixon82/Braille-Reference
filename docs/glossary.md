# Glossary: Braille-Reference

Domain terms used in this project, defined as they are first met. Entries are listed alphabetically. General team terms are in the [AgentTeam global wiki: glossary.md](../../AgentTeam/docs/glossary.md).

## Alphabetic wordsign

A single braille cell that represents a whole word as well as a letter. For example, cell dots 1 stands for both the letter "a" and the word "a". There are 23 alphabetic wordsigns in Unified English Braille Grade 2.

## Braille cell

The basic unit of braille. A braille cell is a rectangular arrangement of up to six raised dots in two columns of three. The dots are numbered 1 to 3 down the left column and 4 to 6 down the right column.

## Contraction

A shortened braille symbol that represents a common word, word part, or letter sequence. Contractions are the defining feature of Grade 2 braille and reduce the space a text takes on the page.

## Dot pattern

A description of which dots are raised in a braille cell or a sequence of cells. Written as the dot numbers separated by commas within a cell, with a slash between cells. For example, "dots 1, 2, 3" describes the cell with the top-left, middle-left, and bottom-left dots raised.

## Final-letter contraction

A braille contraction whose meaning is determined by the letter that follows a prefix indicator. Prefix indicators dots 4, 6 and dots 5, 6 introduce final-letter contractions. There are 12 final-letter contractions in the current data set.

## Grade 1 braille

Uncontracted braille. Every letter, digit, and punctuation mark is spelled out cell by cell, with no contractions. Used where accuracy is essential, such as in mathematics or code.

## Grade 2 braille

Contracted braille. Grade 1 symbols are used together with a set of contractions and shortforms that shorten common words and word parts. Grade 2 is the standard for general reading and writing in English.

## Groupsign

A braille contraction that represents a common letter group, such as "ch", "sh", or "ing", whether that group appears at the start, middle, or end of a word.

## Initial-letter contraction

A braille contraction whose meaning is determined by the letter that follows a prefix indicator. Prefix indicators dot 5, dots 4 and 5, and dots 4, 5, and 6 introduce initial-letter contractions. There are 27 initial-letter contractions in the current data set.

## Lower contraction

A braille contraction formed using only the lower four dots of the cell (dots 2, 3, 5, and 6). Lower contractions serve as both wordsigns and groupsigns depending on their position in a word.

## Numeric indicator

A braille cell (dots 3, 4, 5, 6) that signals the start of a number. It must precede each digit symbol because digits share their cells with letters.

## Shortform

An abbreviated braille spelling of a common word. Shortforms use real braille letters but omit some letters from the full spelling. For example, "ab" stands for "about". There are 17 shortforms in the current data set.

## Strong groupsign

A braille contraction that can represent a letter group in any position within a word: beginning, middle, or end. There are 12 strong groupsigns in Unified English Braille Grade 2.

## Strong wordsign

A braille contraction that represents a complete word and cannot be used in any other context. The five strong wordsigns in Unified English Braille Grade 2 are: and, for, of, the, with.

## Unified English Braille

Unified English Braille (UEB) is the braille code for English adopted across the English-speaking world, including the United Kingdom, Australia, Canada, New Zealand, and the United States. It replaced several earlier English braille codes and became the standard in the United Kingdom in 2015.

## Wordsign

A braille cell that stands for a complete word. A wordsign is written as a single cell standing alone, surrounded by space.
